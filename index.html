Insights about the data/visualization:

  • The data starts off with a narrow width. Other narrow widths, signifying a smaller amount of cases, occur around June 2020 and June 2021. Some of the peak cases occur around January 2021, September 2021, and January 2022. Note that these are local minimums and maximums.

Since the data is distributed across a calender timeline, I assume that the data was calculated at four points throughout the month. Otherwise, I assume that the data is selected as an average of the 7 days around the day in which the data point is calculated.

Design Critique: My first thought is that the choice to make the data a swirl was mesmerizing and forced the reader to further interpret what they were looking at. Some visualizations are easy to read and do not force the reader to think more critically about the patterns. After taking an initial look at the design, the design clearly starts at the center. Dates are clearly labeled. I think the visualization clearly shows that there are peaks and troughs in the number of cases throughout the seasons. It is not entirely clear how often the data points were calculated, and it is initially hard to understand the magnitude of the number of cases from the width metric chosen. After reading the article as well, it looks like the main problem is understanding just how many cases will occur in January 2022. The visualization makes it seem like there will be a large (scary) amount of cases in January 2022, but with the context of the article, it shares that analysts are actually not sure. The visualization makes readers think the omicron variant can become extremely prolific.

Visualization Sketches

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • My first intuition was to make sure that we could clearly see the timeline across 2020 to 2022
  • I chose a simple line graph to depict the growth and reduction in cases over time.
  • I also chose to make sure that the intervals on each of the axes were easily readable and spaced out.
  • Additionally, I added a projected label for January 2022 so that readers know that at the time of the original design, these were suspected numbers.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • I wanted to depict the years of 2020 and 2021 above and below eachother so the readers can understand
  • I also liked reducing the timeline and comparing 2020 and 2021 so that the reader can understand the general increase in cases over time, as well as observe the peaks and troughs.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • I liked the idea of comparing 2020 and 2021, but I wanted to use a bar graph and select certain mnoths of the year in order to enable the reader to more effectively compare the peaks and troughs.
  • I also wanted to use bar graphs so that the reader can effectively compare months throughout the year against 2020 and 2021, with a relevant legend.
  • I think that this is also an opportunity to showcase projections for January 2022. Future iterations of this graph can include an estimated range of cases for January 2022.

Final Visualization Design

Final Writeup

I am trying to convey the aggressive increase in cases in January 2022, as well as the general oscillatory nature of the number of COVID cases throughout the seasons. The data depicts the sum of new confirmed COVID cases for each month, with separations made for each quarter of the year from the start of 2020 through the end of 2022. I chose to use squares for each of the marks because a line did not seem very appropriate given that we were measuring averages throughout each month of the year (although the data yielded new cases for each data of the year). I thought squares would be a solid way to be able to see how many cases it correlates to. I also made each of the squares larger for this reason. I wanted to also use a legend that shows the square color for each month of the year. I thought this would be a cool way for readers to compare square colors to see the rise or correlation between cases and the month of the year across years. With color coded squares, rather than choosing a line graph, readers are able to perceive peaks and troughs and draw quick comparisons across years as they so desire, which facilitate effective communication. A small critique I have is that I think my visualization downplays the continuity of case rising and falling between months. I think the original visualization shows the continuity of cases sharply increasing or decreasing, in a pretty alarming way (the width makes the reader feel like it is being swallowed by the sheer volume of cases almost). This lack of continuity pulls from the outlier that is January 2022 and kind of just makes it seem like it may just be a “rough month” for COVID rather than a moment in time that should be perceived with more care and thought.

I think in my new visualization, it is more clear how often the data points were calculated without overwhelming the reader. It also depicts the data points at fine enough time frames so that the reader understands the trends that we are trying to convey (the peaks and troughs across seasons). Of course, the data I use here are the new confirmed cases for 2020-2022, so the data for January 2022 is confirmed rather than projected. I think the labels for the graphs and captions help convey this. Additionally, I think my visualization also conveys the peaks and troughs in a more traditional way than the swirl in the original figure did, although I do not think one performed better than the other. I just think that for my style choices and how I like to view data personally, I enjoyed seeing the oscillations linearly rather than in a swirl. As a result, I think some fo the tensions and tradeoffs I encountered were dealing with squares as pinpoints rather than presenting the data continuosly. I think readers can pinpoint trends across years, but it takes away from the swift increase and decreasees in the number of cases that I thin the original design does well.
